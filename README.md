## Overview

The Boto Cor-de-Rosa, or pink river dolphin, is a shapeshifting being of Amazonian folklore centered on the Amazon river dolphin Inia geoffrensis. According to traditions held by riverine communities along the Amazon and its tributaries — particularly in the Brazilian states of Amazonas and Pará but extending into Peru, Colombia, Bolivia, and Venezuela — the dolphin emerges from the river at dusk and transforms into a tall, handsome, well-dressed young man. Almost always he wears a hat to conceal the blowhole that does not disappear with the transformation. He attends festas (riverside parties), dances exceptionally well, seduces a young woman, and returns to the water before sunrise. The folklore complex serves several social functions: it provides a culturally legible explanation for unwanted or unexplained pregnancies (a fatherless child is often called a 'filho do boto'), enforces taboos against entering the water alone between dusk and dawn, and frames the boto as one of many encantados — enchanted beings able to move between human and animal form. Within the broader encantado tradition the boto is also credited with controlling storms, causing illness or madness, and drawing humans into the underwater realm. The myth has direct conservation consequences: in some communities reverence and fear protect dolphins, while in others (notably documented since the 2000s in the upper Amazon) dolphin parts have been killed for use as piracatinga catfish bait or sold as love charms.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: 'Boto' is the common Portuguese term for several South American river dolphins; 'cor-de-rosa' is Portuguese for 'pink-coloured', the diagnostic adult pigmentation of Inia geoffrensis. The term 'encantado' ('enchanted one') is the broader category to which the boto belongs in Amazonian folk religion.
- **Meaning**: 'Pink dolphin' / 'pink boto'. The pinkening of older adults is caused by abrasion-related scarring of the skin.
- **First use**: Portuguese-language usage attested in Brazilian folklore collections from the 19th century onward.

- **Coloration**: pink (in dolphin form, intensifying with age), grey-pink, human flesh tones (in transformed form)
- **Morphology**: In its animal form the boto is the freshwater cetacean Inia geoffrensis: an unfused-cervical-vertebra dolphin able to flex its neck, with a long beak, paddle-like flippers, and a low dorsal ridge. In transformed form it is described as an idealized human male; female-boto traditions, in which a woman emerges from the river to seduce men, are also recorded but rarer.

## Distribution

The folklore tracks the natural range of Inia geoffrensis: the Amazon and Orinoco basins of South America. The pink-river-dolphin shapeshifter is most strongly associated with the Brazilian Amazon, with parallel encantado traditions in the Peruvian, Colombian, Bolivian, and Venezuelan Amazon.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Boto as encantado** _(Amazonian riverine communities, Candace Slater (ethnographer of the tradition))_

The traditional Amazonian view: the boto is one form of an encantado, an enchanted being that moves between the underwater otherworld and the world of humans.

### Skeptical

**Social-explanatory function** _(Candace Slater, Mark Cravalho)_

Anthropologists analyse the myth as a culturally legible cover story for unwanted pregnancies and a vehicle for water-safety taboos in environments where drowning is common.

**Misperception of dolphin behaviour** _(Cetacean researchers (e.g., Vera da Silva, Mamirauá Institute))_

Inia geoffrensis is naturally curious, will approach swimmers, and surfaces audibly at night; cetologists argue these behaviours plausibly seed the human-encounter narrative.


## Indigenous Perspectives

> ⚠️ **Encantado traditions are living religious frameworks for many Amazonian communities, not merely 'folklore'.**
